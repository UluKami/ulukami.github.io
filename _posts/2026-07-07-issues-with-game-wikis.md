This is a topic that I just recently had trouble with, but I know has been an issue within this space for a long time. Wikipages and guides that tell confident misinformation.

This can be seen in multiple genres, but the soulslike field is one of the easiest places to point to for one to see this phenomena in action.

The wikipage Fextralife has been in hot water for artificially inflating their views on the live-streaming platform Twitch.tv (see [here](https://www.dexerto.com/entertainment/fextralife-under-fire-by-streamers-for-stealing-views-2268389/)), but they also suffer from proper management. They maintain old information and allow people to upload misleading and blatently wrong information to the site.

I personally experienced this when I was in highschool playing through the Dark Souls franchise. I still remember the grueling experience of using the Fextralife page for finding the Silver Pendant in Oolacile Township, an area in the first game's dlc. The walkthrough on the site had a terrible explanation on where to find it, causing me to go in loops until I watched a video that explained the route used to get to the item. The modern page for this item still has a vague description of how to find the item and better yet, includes misinformation about this item being related to the Path of the Dragon covenant.

This is but one example of this issue, one of the more popular examples are their boss descriptions. Take Dancer of the Boreal Valley from the third game. Her page has a tab that lists the general info of the boss, including a section that lists her weakness as only dark damage. This isn't true. Matter of fact, the wikipage actively contradicts this information in the "Notes & Tips" section of the page, which actively states that she is "weak to dark damage, strike damage, and lightning damage".

Another boss that suffers the same issue is Yhorm the Giant from the third game. His general info tab lists his only weaknesses as lightning and dark damage. But not only is that not the full picture, it is also misleading to how the fight is meant to be fought. The boss arena has a weapon called the Stormruler that is designed for Yhorm to be weak to. The intended strategy for the fight is to use this weapon, which the rest of the wikipage agrees on, but they didn't feel like notifying the user of the page about this in the most eye catching part of the page.

None of this wouldn't be an issue if they weren't one of the biggest sites for categorizing and sharing information about these games. The fact that most people are basing their judgements on a platform that doesn't care to review or fix their mistakes often is exactly why it is a problem. 

But enough of Fextralife, I personally experienced this phenomena recently when I was playing through the hit JRPG Final Fantasy VII. A friend of mine recommended the site Jegged to use as a guide. Now, I want to preface that the site is actually well made and I like using it more than using GameFaqs guides. However, I did notice that one of the pages for a side quest line was wrong.

While I was doing the chocobo raising side quest, I got the wrong attributes for one of my chocobo. The guide recommends the user soft resets the game to rerandomize the stats. I did this for several minutes to no avail. After digging around, I found out the reason for this. The game doesn't rerandomize the seed for the chocobo when you leave the game. Instead, it will follow down a line in the seed based on your actions towards chocobo raising. For example, by feeding any chocobo once, you will reach another stat block for the chocobo you are trying to obtain. If you don't get the stats you want, you would then soft reset and feed a chocobo twice and so on and so forth. But none of this information is on Jegged's guide.

I believe that this all stems from a lack of proper moderation on these sites. I don't blame Jegged for not combing through their entries since the organization is small, but sites such as Fextralife should be held more responsible for maintaining accurate information on their site. Especially when these organizations are big enough in scope to have information slip through the cracks.

Anyway, I just felt like discussing this. I am curious to know if you have experienced this elsewhere or if you don't agree with my standpoint. Thanks for reading!
